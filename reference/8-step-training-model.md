# The Army 8-Step Training Model

> Source: The Lightning Press SMARTbooks — "The Army 8-Step Training Model" (https://www.thelightningpress.com/8-step-training-model/), aligned to ADP/FM 7-0.
> Explanation page (no PDF in this directory).

The 8-Step Training Model is described as "the Army's preeminent training model" for small units at company level and below. It is the systematic framework for **planning, executing, and evaluating** a training event — and it is the cycle that the AAR (step 7) and Retrain (step 8) close.

---

## The eight steps

1. **Plan the training event** — establish measurable objectives aligned with commander's guidance, coordinate resources, allocate time, identify hazards, and develop evaluation plans that support the tasks trained.
2. **Train and certify leaders** — those leading training must be certified, with detailed knowledge of the subject matter and demonstrated proficiency (including certifying opposing-force leaders).
3. **Recon training sites** — verify locations can support the event and resolve logistical issues.
4. **Issue the operation order (OPORD)** — specify responsibilities, timelines, and scenarios so subordinates have all information needed to prepare and execute.
5. **Rehearse** — ensure plans are synchronized and actions are understood by subordinates.
6. **Train (execute)** — execute training with trainers observing and evaluating performance against published standards.
7. **Conduct after action reviews (AARs)** — provide feedback, discuss lessons learned; lessons are recorded for future use and shared with other units and leaders. *(See `fm-7-0-appendix-k-after-action-reviews.md`.)*
8. **Retrain** — tasks not meeting standard are retrained and re-evaluated until the standard is achieved.

---

## How it forms a cycle

The model is explicitly cyclical. Steps 1–6 produce a training event; step 7 (AAR) evaluates it and **signals the start of the next planning cycle** (FM 7-0 K-3); step 8 (Retrain) loops back to execution until the standard is met. Training management as a whole — prioritize → plan/prepare → execute → evaluate/assess → (new round of planning) — is the larger loop this model sits inside.

---

## Why this matters for a closed-loop system

This model is where AAR output is *supposed* to land. FM 7-0 K-13 even assigns "a senior trainer to capture the results of the AAR and to integrate results into training **in accordance with the 8-Step Training Model**."

The loop is therefore designed-closed:
- **Step 7 (AAR)** captures lessons →
- **Step 8 (Retrain)** acts on them →
- **Step 1 (Plan)** of the next cycle incorporates them.

The break in practice is between "lessons recorded in step 7" and "doctrine/playbook/SOP actually updated so step 1 of the *next unit's* cycle reflects them." Step 8 retrains *this* unit, but organization-wide doctrine/playbook revision is not mechanically forced. That gap — capture → track → reflect into the standing playbook — is the NOEMA wedge.
