# FM 7-0, Appendix K — After Action Reviews

> Source: U.S. Army Field Manual FM 7-0 (Training), 14 June 2021, Appendix K.
> Original PDF: `FM 7-0 Appendix K.pdf` (in this directory).
> Authoritative reference: Army Training Network (ATN), https://atn.army.mil

This is the canonical doctrine defining the After Action Review (AAR) — the formal, standards-based feedback procedure that closes the training loop. Captured here verbatim-where-possible for the NOEMA closed-loop training research.

---

## Overview (K-1 to K-4)

**K-1.** An after action review (AAR) is a guided analysis of a Soldier's or organization's performance, conducted at appropriate times during and at the conclusion of a training event or operation with the objective of improving future performance. It includes a facilitator, event participants, and other observers. AARs are conducted at every echelon and are essential in correcting observed training deficiencies by providing feedback that is **immediate, direct, and standards based**.

**K-2.** AARs are a professional discussion requiring the participation of those trained. AARs enable and encourage participants to self-discover what happened then develop a plan for improving task performance. AARs focus on the commander's intent, guidance, training objectives, and task standards. They are **not a critique**, and leaders avoid creating an environment of pointing out failures. The climate of the AAR must encourage candid and open discussion of task performance without stifling learning and team building by—

- Emphasizing meeting the Army standard on tasks rather than judging success or failure.
- Using leading questions to encourage self-discovery and important lessons.
- Allowing a large number of Soldiers and leaders — including opposing forces (OPFORs) — to participate so more lessons are shared.
- Assigning leader responsibility to improve task performance.

**K-3.** AARs also **signal the start of the next planning cycle**. Lessons learned from the review of performance provide leaders the specifics of what and how to perform better for future training. The AAR process ensures participants self-discover what went right, what went wrong, and how to perform to standard next time. Leaders capture AAR results to craft better, more effective training plans and execution. AARs also help leaders frame the unit's **retraining** efforts. To the greatest extent, training is not complete until all training objectives are met. When this is not possible, leaders ensure retraining is planned and executed as expeditiously as possible.

**K-4.** AARs can be either **formal** or **informal**. A formal AAR is resource-intensive and involves planning for and preparing the AAR site, supporting training aids, and supporting personnel. Informal AARs require far less planning and preparation, and they can and should be conducted when unit performance requires it.

---

## Fundamentals of the Training AAR (K-5)

AARs have the following fundamental characteristics. They—

- Are conducted **during or immediately after** each event.
- Focus on commander's intent, guidance, training objectives, and task standards.
- Involve **all participants** in the discussion.
- Use **open-ended questions**.
- Encourage initiative and innovation.
- Determine observed strengths and weaknesses.
- **Link performance to subsequent training.**

---

## Agenda for the Training AAR (K-6 to K-10)

**K-6.** AARs follow an agenda, which includes—

1. A review of **what was supposed to happen**.
2. Establishing **what did happen**.
3. Determining **what was right or wrong** with what happened.
4. Determining **how to perform the task to standard next time**.

- **K-7. What was supposed to happen** — Facilitator/evaluator and participants review the plan, based on the commander's guidance, training objectives, and tasks to train.
- **K-8. What happened** — Facilitator and participants jointly determine what actually occurred. The leader gathers as many perspectives as feasible (OPFOR, squad leader, team leader, rifleman) to establish a common, accurate account of events.
- **K-9. What was right or wrong** — Participants identify strong and weak points against the commander's guidance and performance measures. Guided discussion reaches conclusions that are doctrinally sound, consistent with Army standards, and relevant to the mission.
- **K-10. How to do it differently next time** — The unit self-determines how to perform more effectively, identifies problems, provides solutions, **and identifies who is responsible for making the recommended changes**.

## The Four-Step AAR Process

> **K-12.** "The AAR process has four steps: Plan. Execute. Evaluate. Integrate and share lessons learned."

### Step 1 — Plan (K-13 to K-30)

Commanders provide guidance to develop an AAR plan for each training event. The guidance applies to formal and informal AARs and identifies—

- Who conducts the AAR.
- Who provides information.
- Aspects of the operation an AAR evaluates.
- Who attends the AAR.
- When and where the AAR occurs.
- **A senior trainer to capture the results of the AAR and to integrate results into training in accordance with the 8-Step Training Model.** *(← this is the loop-closing role)*

Planning sub-tasks:
- **Selecting and training evaluators (K-15–16)** — choose leaders/evaluators who have demonstrated task proficiency, know the duties they are observing, and know current doctrine. External evaluators are at least equal in rank; if forced to choose, pick experience over rank. Commanders are responsible for training and certifying evaluators, including training on conducting AARs.
- **Reviewing training and evaluation outlines (K-17)** — review the applicable outlines to understand task requirements and standards.
- **Scheduling stopping points (K-18)** — plan AARs during and at the end of each critical phase or major event.
- **Determining attendance (K-19)** — at squad/platoon level, everyone attends; at company+ it may be only key players and commanders.
- **Choosing training aids (K-20)** — use an aid only if it makes the AAR better.
- **Reviewing the AAR plan (K-21)** — the plan is only a guide; adjust as needed.
- **Using facilitators (K-22–23)** — internal or external; they record events, actions, and observations by time sequence to prevent loss of feedback.
- **Selecting AAR sites (K-24–25)** — at/near the training site, ideally with view of the terrain where critical actions occurred.
- **Collecting observations (K-26–27)** — gather a complete picture of what happened.
- **Organizing the AAR (K-28–29)** — sequence by one of three techniques: chronological order of events, warfighting functions, or key events/themes/issues.
- **Rehearsing (K-30)** — facilitators rehearse delivery of the AAR.

### Step 2 — Execute (K-31 to K-40)

The AAR begins when training stops and key players assemble. Purpose: participants **discover strengths and weaknesses, propose solutions, and adopt actions to improve performance**.

1. **Provide an introduction and rules (K-32–33)** — everyone participates; it is candid and professional, measured against task standards (not a critique). Atmosphere guidance: permit respectful disagreement, focus on learning, encourage honest opinions, use open-ended/leading questions, enter the discussion only when necessary.
2. **State the agenda (K-34–35)** — review training objectives; restate tasks, conditions, and standards; restate the mission, guidance, and concept of operations.
3. **Summarize what happened (K-36–37)** — reconstruct the event from multiple perspectives using open-ended (not yes/no) questions. The facilitator does **not** tell Soldiers what was good or bad; participants reveal the issues.
4. **Identify what needs improvement or sustainment (K-38)** — discuss successes and failures against the mission and performance measures. Concentrate on what went wrong, **not on the person responsible** — refer to "the platoon leader," not "2LT Wilson."
5. **Determine how the task should be done differently (K-39)** — find a more effective method and a more effective way to train it.
6. **Provide closing comments (K-40)** — summarize key points, end on a constructive note, link conclusions to future training; the facilitator then leaves so the unit can debrief privately.

### Step 3 — Evaluate (K-41)

AARs are a critical tool to evaluate training. Soldiers compare performance against the standard and identify specific ways to improve proficiency. **The benefits of AARs come from applying results in developing future training.** Commanders use AAR results to assess unit performance and plan future training.

### Step 4 — Integrate and Share Lessons Learned (K-42 to K-44)

> This is the loop-closing step — and the one most often dropped in practice.

**K-42.** Implementing and sharing lessons learned is an important last step. Commanders **must** use AARs and lessons learned to shape training and operations to correct deficiencies and sustain superior performance. **Lessons learned from the AAR process are always recorded.** At higher echelons, results and lessons learned are shared with other units and the Army in the **after action report**.

**K-43.** After action reports are organized in logical, chronological order (by phase or warfighting function). Documenting **what did not work well**, and the actions established to correct future performance, should receive as much attention as what did work well.

**K-44.** Report format/content is at the commander's discretion. Reports are retained by the unit, periodically reviewed and discussed by leaders, and provided to the next higher commander for comment and possible dissemination.

---

## Why this matters for a closed-loop system

The doctrine already defines a closed loop: **Plan → Execute → Evaluate → Integrate/Share → (retrain + next planning cycle)**. Two control points are explicitly assigned but fragile in practice:

1. **K-13 senior-trainer role** — someone must capture AAR results and integrate them per the 8-Step Training Model. If unassigned or unsupported, the loop opens.
2. **K-42 "always recorded" + K-44 "retained, reviewed, disseminated"** — the doctrinal requirement exists, but tracking-to-closure (lessons → doctrine/SOP/playbook change) is the step that, absent a system, leaves lessons "on the shelf."

A NOEMA-style layer that **automatically captures, tracks to closure, and reflects lessons back into doctrine/playbooks** sits exactly on K-13 and K-42–44.
