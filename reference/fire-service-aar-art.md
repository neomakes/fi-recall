# The Art of the After-Action Review (Fire Service)

> Source: Adam LaReau & Brice Long, "The Art of the After-Action Review," Fire Engineering, May 1, 2018 (https://www.fireengineering.com/firefighting/the-art-of-the-after-action-review/).
> Explanation page (no PDF in this directory).

The fire-service practitioner counterpart to FM 7-0: how to actually run an AAR (a.k.a. "hot wash" / debrief) on the fireground, and — critically — the human obstacles that break the learning loop.

---

## Purpose

An AAR is a systematic analysis of performance across all phases of an operation, from predispatch through cleanup. The fundamental goal: **"reduce the number of the same mistakes made twice and encourage repeat wins."** AARs are experience multipliers — they let one person benefit from many perspectives, accelerating organizational learning. The practice originates in military Special Operations culture.

## The four key questions

Every AAR addresses:
1. What was supposed to happen?
2. What actually happened?
3. Why did it happen that way?
4. What will we do to improve the way we do it next time?

*(Note the 1:1 correspondence with FM 7-0 K-6.)*

## Chronological review components

Debrief sequentially: Call → Preplanning → Training → Dispatch/Response → On-Scene → Under Control/Overhaul → Cleanup/Decon → Return to Service → Outcomes → **Lessons Learned** → Reporting → Comments.

---

## Psychological safety and culture (the make-or-break factor)

- **Mindset prerequisite** — participants need a growth mindset and willingness for objective self-reflection.
- **Flatten rank** — conduct debriefs "as flat as possible, with participants checking their ranks at the door." All perspectives matter; the new firefighter may have seen what the chief did not, and vice versa.
- **Trust** — the process depends on communication, trust, and honesty, focused on evolution.

### Fear-based barriers (why honest input gets suppressed)
- **Veterans/leaders** fear exposure of mistakes damaging reputation/authority.
- **Probationary members** fear appearing inexperienced among peers.
- **Mid-career firefighters** carry both anxieties, protecting accumulated standing.

### Ground rules to overcome them
- **Focus on situations, not individuals** — describe the action and what caused the mistake, not who made it. *(Matches FM 7-0 K-38: "the platoon leader," not "2LT Wilson.")*
- **Own your perspective** — use "I" statements.
- **Avoid group-think** — rotate participation; the loudest voices may need to yield the floor.
- **Respect diverse viewpoints** — pump operators see things differently from entry teams.
- **Distinguish fault from responsibility** — "Fault places blame and injects excuses... responsibility fosters ownership and acceptance."
- **Avoid checkbox compliance** — sessions without critical thinking provide no benefit.
- **Control the environment** — distraction-free space for all participants.

---

## Translating insights to action (the loop-closing step)

> "The AAR teases out the action items; the next step is to put what was learned into action."

- Leaders are responsible for ensuring **feedback becomes actionable training** to reinforce wins and correct losses.
- **Lessons learned should be distributed** so other members, companies, shifts, and departments benefit.
- Success requires **repeating the process systematically** — for all operations, incidents and training, wins and losses.
- Templates build familiarity but should stay dynamic and flexible.

---

## Why this matters for a closed-loop system

This article names the two failure modes a closed-loop system must engineer around:

1. **Suppressed honesty** (low psychological safety) — the input side breaks before lessons are even captured. A system that de-personalizes capture (situation, not person) and lowers the social cost of reporting directly addresses this.
2. **Action items that never become action** — "put what was learned into action" and "distribute lessons learned" are stated as duties but rely on individual leader follow-through. Without tracking, action items stall — the same "sit on the shelf" failure as the formal report.

The NOEMA wedge sits on both: lower the cost/threat of honest capture, then **track each action item to closure and reflect it into the standing playbook/SOP** so the next crew inherits the lesson.
